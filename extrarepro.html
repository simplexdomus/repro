The Molecular Basis of Life

Understanding how molecules interact to create the complex systems we call living organisms

Information

Genetic information flows from DNA to RNA to proteins, following the central dogma of molecular biology. This unidirectional flow ensures accurate transmission of hereditary information.

Structure

Molecular structure determines function. The three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in biological molecules dictates their interactions and roles within the cell.

Energy

Life requires constant energy input. ATP serves as the universal energy currency, powering everything from muscle contraction to DNA replication.

DNA Structure

The double helix — nature's most elegant information storage system

The Blueprint of Life

Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is a polymer composed of nucleotide units. Each nucleotide contains a phosphate group, a deoxyribose sugar, and one of four nitrogenous bases.

The two strands run antiparallel to each other, with the 5' end of one strand opposite the 3' end of the other. This orientation is crucial for DNA replication and transcription.

Hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs hold the two strands together: Adenine pairs with Thymine via two hydrogen bonds, while Guanine pairs with Cytosine via three hydrogen bonds.

03

The RNA World

Versatile molecules that serve as messengers, catalysts, and regulators

mRNA

Messenger RNA

Carries genetic information from DNA to ribosomes. Each mRNA molecule encodes the amino acid sequence for a specific protein. Codons (three-nucleotide sequences) specify which amino acid to incorporate.

→ Template for protein synthesis

tRNA

Transfer RNA

Small adapter molecules that translate the genetic code. Each tRNA carries a specific amino acid and recognizes the corresponding mRNA codon through its anticodon sequence.

→ Amino acid delivery to ribosomes

rRNA

Ribosomal RNA

The catalytic core of ribosomes. rRNA molecules form complex three-dimensional structures that catalyze peptide bond formation between amino acids during translation.

→ Structural and catalytic ribosome component

miRNA

MicroRNA

Small non-coding RNAs (~22 nucleotides) that regulate gene expression post-transcriptionally. They bind to complementary sequences in target mRNAs, leading to translational repression or mRNA degradation.

→ Gene expression regulation

lncRNA

Long Non-coding RNA

Transcripts longer than 200 nucleotides that don't encode proteins. They play diverse roles in chromatin remodeling, transcriptional regulation, and nuclear organization.

→ Epigenetic and transcriptional control

Protein Synthesis

From genetic code to functional molecules

1

Initiation

Ribosome assembles on mRNA at the start codon (AUG). Initiator tRNA carrying methionine binds.

2

Elongation

tRNAs bring amino acids. Peptide bonds form between adjacent amino acids. Ribosome moves along mRNA.

3

Termination

Stop codon reached (UAA, UAG, UGA). Release factors trigger polypeptide release.

4

Folding

Polypeptide folds into functional 3D structure. Chaperones assist proper folding.

5

Modification

Post-translational modifications add functional groups, cleave segments, or attach molecules.

The Cell Cycle

Orchestrated phases of growth, replication, and division

G1

Gap 1 Phase

Cell growth and preparation for DNA synthesis. The cell increases in size and synthesizes proteins needed for S phase.

S

Synthesis Phase

DNA replication occurs. Each chromosome is duplicated, resulting in sister chromatids joined at the centromere.

G2

Gap 2 Phase

Final preparations for mitosis. The cell continues to grow and produces proteins required for chromosome separation.

M

Mitotic Phase

Nuclear division (mitosis) and cytoplasmic division (cytokinesis). Results in two genetically identical daughter cells.

06

Cellular Machinery

Specialized compartments that perform essential cellular functions

Nucleus

The control center of the cell, housing the genetic material. Double membrane with nuclear pores allows selective transport of molecules.

Contains approximately 2 meters of DNA packaged into 46 chromosomes

Mitochondria

Powerhouses of the cell, producing ATP through oxidative phosphorylation. Contain their own circular DNA and ribosomes.

Generate about 90% of cellular ATP through the electron transport chain

Endoplasmic Reticulum

Network of membranes for protein (rough ER) and lipid (smooth ER) synthesis. Connected to the nuclear envelope.

Rough ER can have up to 13 million ribosomes per cell

Golgi Apparatus

Processing and packaging center. Modifies proteins and lipids, sorts them, and packages them for transport.

Processes approximately 10,000 protein molecules per minute

Lysosomes

Digestive compartments containing hydrolytic enzymes. Break down macromolecules, old organelles, and foreign materials.

Maintain an acidic pH of 4.5-5.0 for optimal enzyme activity

Ribosomes

Molecular machines for protein synthesis. Composed of rRNA and proteins, existing free or bound to the ER.

A single cell can contain up to 10 million ribosomes

Peroxisomes

Metabolic compartments for oxidation reactions. Detoxify harmful substances and metabolize fatty acids.

Can replicate by division and are imported with peroxisomal proteins

Cytoskeleton

Dynamic network of protein filaments. Provides structure, enables movement, and organizes cellular components.

Composed of microtubules, actin filaments, and intermediate filaments
07

Cell Signaling

How cells communicate and respond to their environment

Autocrine

Cell signals to itself. The signaling molecule binds to receptors on the same cell that produced it.

Paracrine

Local signaling to nearby cells. Signal molecules diffuse short distances to reach neighboring target cells.

Endocrine

Long-distance signaling via bloodstream. Hormones travel throughout the body to reach distant target cells.

Synaptic

Neural signaling across synapses. Neurotransmitters cross synaptic clefts to signal adjacent neurons.

08

Epigenetics

Heritable changes beyond the DNA sequence

Beyond the Genetic Code

Epigenetics studies heritable changes in gene expression that occur without alterations to the DNA sequence. These modifications act as an additional layer of information on top of the genetic code.

Unlike genetic mutations, epigenetic changes are potentially reversible and can be influenced by environmental factors such as diet, stress, and exposure to toxins.

Epigenetic patterns are established during development and can be maintained through cell division. They play crucial roles in cell differentiation, X-chromosome inactivation, and genomic imprinting.

Dysregulation of epigenetic mechanisms has been implicated in various diseases, including cancer, neurological disorders, and autoimmune conditions.

DNA Methylation

Addition of methyl groups to cytosine bases, typically at CpG dinucleotides. Generally associated with gene silencing when present in promoter regions.

Histone Modification

Chemical modifications to histone proteins (acetylation, methylation, phosphorylation) that alter chromatin structure and gene accessibility.

Chromatin Remodeling

ATP-dependent complexes that reposition nucleosomes, making DNA more or less accessible to transcription machinery.

Non-coding RNAs

Various RNA molecules that regulate gene expression through RNA interference, chromatin modification, and other mechanisms.

Milestones of Discovery

Key moments in the history of molecular biology

1869

DNA Discovered

Friedrich Miescher isolates "nuclein" from white blood cells, the substance we now know as DNA.

1944

DNA as Genetic Material

Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty demonstrate that DNA, not protein, carries genetic information.

1953

Double Helix Structure

Watson and Crick propose the double helix model for DNA structure, based on X-ray data from Franklin and Wilkins.

1958

Central Dogma

Francis Crick articulates the central dogma of molecular biology: DNA → RNA → Protein.

1961

Genetic Code Cracked

Nirenberg and Matthaei decipher the first codon (UUU for phenylalanine), beginning to crack the genetic code.

1977

DNA Sequencing

Sanger and Gilbert independently develop methods for sequencing DNA, revolutionizing molecular biology.

1983

PCR Invented

Kary Mullis develops the polymerase chain reaction, enabling amplification of specific DNA sequences.

2003

Human Genome Complete

The Human Genome Project completes sequencing of the human genome, identifying ~20,000 genes.

2012

CRISPR-Cas9

Doudna and Charpentier demonstrate CRISPR-Cas9 as a programmable gene-editing tool.
